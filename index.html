Interests

Concurrency and Parallelism

The idea of being able to perform a task more efficiently by somehow dividing up the workload has always held a strong appeal for me, and since taking my first steps into implementing concurrency, it's become one of my favorite ways to improve runtime. Implementing it often proves difficult on multiple levels, providing unique challenges that keep me on my toes. I also appreciate that it presents opportunities for making different design decisions, from leaning into the massively parallelized architecture of a GPU, with its unique strengths and weaknesses, to rebuilding a data-processing function from the ground up to pipeline individual objects so they can be handled by different cores or processors.

Algorithm Optimization

Just like concurrency and parallelism, algorithm optimization provides the ability to make a program run more efficiently, and that's why I enjoy it. Perhaps there's a bottleneck that could be removed, or maybe a program makes unnecessary passes over a data set. When dealing with algorithms, there are infinite possibilities that can be explored, particularly as the problem's complexity increases, so problems can stay varied and feel fresh even after encountering hundreds of them.

UX and Design

This topic might seem to not match up with the rest of this list, but I've always appreciated intuitive and beautifully-designed applications, so once I started to get opportunities to try my own hand at application development, I found that designing programs to meet those qualifications is even more gratifying than using them! There's a certain je ne sais quoi about hitting "debug" and having an easy to use, pleasing to the eye, and intuitive to navigate program appear in front of me, the product of too many late nights either spent in front of a monitor or in bed sleeplessly staring through the ceiling. It combines the satisfaction of making something beautiful with that of making something that works, and that makes the countless hours poured into both back and front-end design worth it.

Documentation

It seems that I never got the memo that I'm supposed to hate writing documentation - I think it can be pretty fun! Everyone I've talked to, from students to professors to even a Googler, has expressed that documentation is something that they avoid, more of a chore to be shirked than something actually interesting to them. For me, it's an opportunity to smooth the experience for other developers and also expand my own knowledge on a topic. For example, I might know my way around a project well enough to push code, but if you asked me to explain it off the top of my head to someone with no prior knowledge of the project, there's a good possibility that I'd be at a loss to describe what's going on and how the program works. If I'm able to explain it, however --- or even better, write a comprehensive guide that fully informs another developer new to the team --- I can be fairly confident that I understand the whole thing inside and out, and that will improve my own contributions as well as empower others in the long run.

Big Data

The main reason I like the idea of working with big data is that any improvement made has the potential to be magnified astronomically by the scale of the task at hand. At that scale, mathematically small tweaks to efficiency or runtime can become worth chasing down thanks to their effect being upscaled to become practically significant. That means new opportunities for interesting problems to solve and insights to uncover while still making a difference at the end of the day.
